## The Situation

You have been told to block kicks with your shin. Lift the knee, turn the leg, let the bone take the impact.

Then you watch Tawanchai PK Saenchai — Lumpinee champion, one of the most technically correct fighters alive — break his shin blocking a kick in a professional fight.

Same technique. Same instruction you have been given. His bone snapped.

If it happens to him, what are you supposed to do with that information?

## Your Turn

Find the clip of the moment. Watch it at normal speed once. Then find a way to slow it down — 0.25x if you can.

Do not watch the kick. Watch where the kick lands on his shin. The exact point of contact on the bone.

Then find three or four blocks from other fights that do not break. Any fighter. Watch the same thing — the exact point of contact, the angle of the shin at the moment of impact.

Come back when you have seen what is different between the blocks that hold and the one that did not.

---

*The answer is in the bone itself.*

## Solution

Your shin is not equally strong along its entire length.

Run your thumb down the front of your shin from knee to ankle. Feel where the bone narrows — there is a section in the middle where the tibia is at its thinnest. Not the top. Not the bottom near the ankle. A specific point roughly mid-shin where the bone has less thickness, less density than the rest.

That is the weak point. It exists on every fighter's shin. It existed on Tawanchai's.

**What a skilled fighter knows**

High-level fighters know where that spot is — on their own shin and on their opponent's. They have spent years training their kick to land in specific places. Kicking the thick lower shin of someone who blocks well hurts the kicker. Kicking the thin part mid-shin, at the right angle with the right force, breaks the block.

When Tawanchai's shin broke, the kick did not find a tired fighter or a badly positioned block. It found the weak point. That is not luck. A fighter who can consistently target that specific section on a moving, raised shin in a live fight has developed exceptional precision.

**What conditioning actually does**

Conditioning your shin — the rolling bottles, the hard sparring, the years of impact — builds bone density. The repeated low-level stress stimulates the bone to grow denser and harder throughout the shin.

Including the thin part.

So conditioning does not remove the weakness. It raises how much force is needed to reach the breaking point. On an unconditioned shin, much less is required. On Tawanchai's shin — twenty years of professional training — it required a fighter precise enough to find exactly the right spot.

This is why conditioning matters even though it could not fully protect Tawanchai. Without it, the same kick lands in the same spot and the break is far easier.

**What to do with this**

Know where your weak point is. Feel it on your own shin right now — the section where the bone is narrowest.

When you block, your goal is not simply to put your shin in the way. It is to position the stronger parts of your shin toward the incoming kick and take the impact away from that narrow section. The block is not one movement. Inside that movement is a question of which part of the bone meets the strike.

And know that a fighter who understands this is watching for that spot every time you raise your leg.
