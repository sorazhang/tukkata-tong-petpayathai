## The Situation

You have been told to block kicks with your shin. Lift the knee, turn the leg, let the bone take the impact.

Then you watch Tawanchai PK Saenchai — Lumpinee champion, one of the most technically correct fighters alive — break his shin blocking a kick in a professional fight.

Same technique. Same instruction you have been given. His bone snapped.

If it happens to him, what are you supposed to do with that information?

## Your Turn

Find the clip of the moment. Watch it at normal speed once. Then find a way to slow it down — 0.25x if you can.

Do not watch the kick. Watch where the kick lands on his shin. The exact point of contact on the bone.

Then find three or four blocks from other fights that do not break. Any fighter. Watch the same thing — the exact point of contact, the angle of the shin at the moment of impact.

Come back when you have seen what is different between the blocks that hold and the one that did not.

---

*The answer is in the geometry, not the bone.*

## Solution

The shin is not a wall. It is a deflection.

When you block a kick correctly, you are not absorbing the force — you are redirecting it. Your shin meets the incoming kick at an angle that breaks the power apart and sends it sideways. The kicker feels that impact in their own leg. You feel almost nothing.

When Tawanchai's shin broke, the deflection did not happen. Two forces met each other directly, head on. At that point it becomes a question of physics — angles, velocity, the exact spot where bone meets bone. That moment, the numbers did not favour him.

**There are two ways to use the shin**

The first is a *block*. The kick is coming, you lift your leg to meet it. Your shin is passive, waiting. The force of the kick determines how much load your bone takes. If the timing is late and the angle is flat, you are absorbing. The kick decides what happens next.

The second is a *check*. You move your shin into the path of the kick before it arrives — angled outward, so the kicker's foot strikes the face of your shin rather than the middle of it. The angle changes what happens to the force. They absorb it, not you.

Watch any fight where a check works well. The kicker hops. Sometimes they grab their foot. They are the one in pain. That is not the bone being stronger. That is the angle being correct.

What happened to Tawanchai was a block that arrived late. The angle was wrong. His shin met the kick flat instead of oblique. There was nowhere for the force to go.

**What this means for your training**

The instruction *block with your shin* is correct. But it is half the instruction.

The full instruction is: meet the kick with your shin, angled so it deflects, timed so you are moving into it before it reaches full power. The angle and the timing are not refinements you add later. They are the reason the technique exists.

Conditioning your shin matters. A dense bone is harder to break. But a well-angled check with an unconditioned shin does less damage than a flat block with a conditioned one. Conditioning does not replace mechanics. It supports them.

Tawanchai has blocked ten thousand kicks correctly. You saw one that was not. Do not let that one change what you understand about the other ten thousand.
