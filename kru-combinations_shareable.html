Long Range — Control & Measure

Jab → Cross → Teep

Long range · Safe entry · Tests the guard
Question
"Why jab-cross-teep instead of going straight to the body kick?"
Kru
"The jab-cross isn't the attack. It's the question. The teep is the answer."
The jab-cross checks if their hands come up. If they do — the teep finds the body open. If they don't — the cross scores. The teep resets distance: don't come in. Nothing here is decoration. Every move has a purpose.

Rhythm break · Frustrate · Distance as a weapon
Question
"What's the point of teeping and stepping back — am I just running?"
Kru
"You're not running. You're changing the beat. The second teep lands when they think you've given them space."
Fighters get into rhythms. They start to time you. The step back breaks the expectation — they think you're retreating, they start to step in, and the second teep meets them halfway. This is how you use distance as a weapon, not just safety.

Pattern building · High value score · Patience required
Question
"Why repeat the body kick before going to the head? Why not go for the head kick straight away?"
Kru
"You're not throwing the same kick three times. You're training their arms to come down."
Two body kicks make the guard drift low — the arms get used to protecting the ribs. The third kick goes where the guard no longer is. The first two aren't scoring techniques. They're coaching the opponent to make a mistake.

Question
"What's the teep doing at the end of an attack? Isn't that the moment to keep going?"
Kru
"You scored. Now you leave. The teep is the full stop at the end of the sentence."
The teep resets distance, kills their momentum, and returns you to safety. Score — then make them come to you again. This is how you win rounds, not just exchanges.

Deception · Must be earned · Timing
Question
"Faking always feels obvious when I do it. When does it actually work?"
Kru
"A feint works when they've already seen the real thing. Teach them to respect the kick first. Then fake it."
You need to have already landed that kick for the fake to cause a real reaction. Deception is built on a history of truth. Throw the real kick many times first. Then the feint becomes a weapon.

The best scoring moments come after the opponent attacks. Countering requires patience, reading, and knowing exactly what to do in the small window you're given.

Question
"Why not just sweep when you catch the kick? That's what everyone expects."
Kru
"The sweep is predictable. Everyone trains for it. The cross first — now they're thinking about the punch while the kick comes."
Catching a kick gives one free second of imbalance. The cross disrupts guard AND thinking. The body kick arrives to a mind processing two problems at once. Always give them two things to deal with. They can only solve one.

Question
"I know I should slip but I always block instead. How do I make slipping feel natural?"
Kru
"Drill the slip not as defence — drill it as the first step of the counter. If the counter doesn't come, you didn't really slip."
The slip alone is worthless. It only has value when it puts you in position to counter. Drill slip → cross as one single movement. The defence and the offence are the same action.

Question
"This seems too simple. Surely they see the teep coming after I block?"
Kru
"They see it. But they're still resetting from the attack. Seeing it and stopping it are different things."
Right after an attack, the opponent's mind is registering failure, their body is recovering. The teep catches that window. The kick follows while they're still adjusting. Simple combos win in Muay Thai. Complicated ones create their own problems.

The clinch is where Muay Thai becomes chess. Outside is about power and distance. Inside is about control, balance, and position. These combos are for when staying outside isn't working.

Question
"When do you choose to go into the clinch instead of staying outside?"
Kru
"When they're bigger and stronger at range. You take away their space. Make the fight yours."
The jab covers entry into close range. The elbow scores and occupies their defence simultaneously. The knee drives while their arms are busy. Outside Muay Thai is power fighting. Clinch Muay Thai is chess. Use it when they're winning the power game.

Question
"How do I get a clean kick after the clinch? Every time I separate I'm off-balance."
Kru
"The push creates the distance. The kick uses their stumble. You don't separate and then kick — you separate with the kick."
Two knees weaken the body and occupy attention. The push exits while also sending them backward — that stumble is a half-second of imbalance. The kick arrives in exactly that window. The exit and the attack are the same movement.

These two are Kru's own. Not from a textbook — from thousands of rounds finding what actually works. The principle in both is the same: two jabs create a predictable reaction. Then you control that reaction.

Question
"Walk me through this step by step — why two jabs first?"
Kru
"The first jab measures the distance. The second commits them to block right. Now I know exactly where their hand is going."
Step by step:
Jab → Jab — forces the right block. You've created a predictable reaction.
Left hand catches their blocking arm and drops it down, opening the centreline.
Punch enters the gap while their guard is controlled by your left hand.
Right knee drives into a body that can no longer protect itself.
Move left — the key step. Already angled and loaded for the kick before they've recovered.
Kick is the natural conclusion. The exit angle was built into the combo from the beginning. The move left is not after the combo — it is the combo.

Right side · Off-balance · Turn opponent · Kru's personal sequence
Question
"What's the difference between this and just catching and sweeping? Why go to the trouble of turning them?"
Kru
"A sweep takes them down. A turn takes away their whole body. They're facing the wrong direction. Everything they have is pointed away from you."
Step by step:
Jab → Jab — draws the left block. Same opening as the left combo.
Left hand catches their blocking arm on the right side of their body.
Right hand pushes the right side simultaneously — catching one side, pushing the other rotates them.
The turn — off-balance and rotating, they cannot punch, cannot kick, cannot block.
Punch arrives while they're still turning — they can't see it coming.
Kick lands on a body still reorganising.
This is not a power combo. It's a control combo. The damage comes from the position, not the force.
🔑 The shared principle
"Both combos start the same. Two jabs. Opponent blocks. Now you own that block. The question is what you do with it."

The left combo drops the block and enters the centreline. The right combo uses the block to rotate the whole body. Same opening — completely different problems for the opponent. Once they've seen one, they don't know which is coming. That uncertainty is the third weapon.

The thinking behind all the techniques. These principles apply to every combo on this page — and every combo not yet invented. If you understand these, you can build your own.

Kru's Core Principles
PrincipleWhat Kru SaysWhat It Means in Practice
Every move has a purpose "Nothing is decoration." If you can't explain why a move is in a combination, it shouldn't be there. Each strike is either scoring, setting up, or controlling.
Create the reaction, then use it "Don't wait for an opening. Make one." Every jab, feint, or teep is a question asked to the opponent. Champions don't react to openings — they engineer them.
Two problems beat one defence "Always give them two things at once. They can only solve one." A block handles one attack. It can't handle two simultaneous ones. The cross after catching a kick works because the mind processes one threat at a time.
The exit is part of the combo "Score — then make them come to you again." Attackers who stay in range after scoring get countered. The teep out, the step left, the push — these are built in from the start, not added at the end.
Deception must be earned "Feints only work after the real thing." A feint not preceded by the genuine technique does nothing. Teach the opponent to respect it first.
Position over power "The damage comes from the position, not the force." Turning an opponent, angling after the knee, catching and controlling — these create damage without extra power. Good position multiplies every technique.
